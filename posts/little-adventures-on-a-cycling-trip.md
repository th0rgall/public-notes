When I tell people about my bicycle travels, I often see them  wondering, "Why would you do such a thing?". 

For me, an important reason are the countless little adventures you bump into on the way. Here is an account of my favorite crazy moments from a 10-day solo cycling trip through France. If you'd enjoy these, maybe consider going on a bicycle tour as well! 

## Day 1 - Crossing a plowed field

![road.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/road.jpg)

It was a cold, rainy day and the sun had just set. My destination, Liège, was still about 10km ahead. I had left late and I was longing for food and dry clothes. The village street I was riding in changed into a bumpy hiking path. Oh well. It went slow, but was feasible.

Then the road suddenly stopped, right in the middle of a plowed and thus extremely muddy field.


![stop.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/stop.jpg)

Directions: go right through.

![mud.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/mud.jpg)

The first day and I was already covered with mud. Yay!

## Day 2 - Camping next to a hardcore festival

Ahh, beautiful: a quiet camping place.

![plain.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/plain.jpg)

Or, not reallly. I arrived at the camping [Oos Heem](https://www.campingoosheem.be/nl/) in East Belgium off-season. After having found the proprietor to check in, he told me there would be a festival pre-party that night in one the campings' buildings. The heavy guitars and electro beats in the background proved his words, until about 3AM. You could say I enjoyed the music for free from my tent! 

![poster.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/poster.jpg)

Oh by the way, when you would have turned around at the first image, you'd have seen a playground with giant chess pieces, two swimming pools and other curiosities.

![behind.jpg](./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/behind.jpg)

## Day 5 - Meeting the same guy twice

This was a funny coincidence that requires a little background story.

After cycling a hefty 120km through Luxembourg's hills that day, I arrived in Thionville at about 8pm. I didn't want my hosts in Metz 35km further down to receive me only at 10pm, so I decided to take the train. I could however not find a way to get a train ticket for my bike from the machine. On the platform I spotted an absent-looking guy wearing big headphones and a black hoodie from some kind of metal band. Surely, he could help me. He told me bikes could be taken free of charge on the local train. Awesome!

In the 20 minute train ride we had a little conversation. We continued our conversation. We had some things in common! He was a computer science student in the same year as I am. We talked about programming languages and atmospheric black metal bands. When we arrived in Metz, he helped me to get my bike out of the station. There we parted ways after exchanging contact information.

## Day 6 - The cycling choir-leader

While cruising through the hilly fields and barely populated towns of rural Grand-Est, I crossed the paths of a man who turned out to be a fellow Belgian citizen! We were following the same route, so we cycled together for the rest of the day.

## Day 6 - A gloomy swim in a water park

We arrived in a village called Mittersheim during a rainy sunset. The village lay next to a lake, where we found a camping. I went to take some pictures of the sunset in the lake, pitched the tent and cooked dinner. I had spotted a funny collection of water attractions, so after dinner I set out to climb these things. Some German camping-goers had the same idea. It was fun.

## Day 7 - Invited to tea by a confused great-granny

The next morning my tablet/GPS got stolen on the camping. My schedule got somewhat mixed-up; I had literally lost my way and, temporarily, also my faith in humanity.

This was quickly regained around 6pm in a village called Mittelbronn a little further. I was taking a picture of an abandoned restaurant when I heard a window opening behind me. An old woman was curious of the strange appearance of my bike. After a short conversation with heavy use of gestures, she invited me in for tea. I hesitantly accepted, the city I wanted to reach was still 20km ahead.

I entered the smelly home full of handcrafted woodwork. Even the fridge was behind a wooden door. She showed me around and told me stories of her late husband and great-grandchildren. She gave me a tea bag, but awkwardly, she didn't remember how to make tea. She didn't appear to remember much at all really. I wondered how she could live by herself this way. Apparently her daughter assisted her during the day and left her by herself in the evenings.

<video controls src="./assets/little-adventures-on-a-cycling-trip/granny_compilation.mp4"></video>

I resumed my journey with a piece of bread, a madeleine, an unused teabag, and a smile on my face. Faith in humanity restored.

## Day 8 - Camping in a school

Ad-hoc planning made me choose campings based on their location. The listing in the GPS only included names and no reviews nor ratings. This makes for unexpected places every time! This municipal camping in Barr was just plain weird: its buildings were fused with a primary school. You had to access the sanitary through the school playground.

## Day 9 - Passing a stretch of unlit corn fields

This was the only moment that felt genuinely scary. Again, I was still cycling after dusk. The camping I want to reach was only 5 kilometers away, but was separated from me by a huge stretch of unlit corn fields.

## Day 9 - Getting smuggled into a full camping

When I reached the camping (around 9PM), I found that the office was already full. This is usually not a problem, just enter, pitch the tent and pay the next day. When entering however, two Dutch women who told me there was no space left. A few people were already sent away earlier that day. But they pitied me and invited me cram my tent next to theirs. 

In order not to get them into trouble, I woke up at 4:30 AM. I quickly packed and sneaked out. Again cycling in the dark, but now before dawn. It was nice to see the sun rise.

## Day 10 - A Crazy Russian near Basel

The evening of the final day a touring cyclist overtook me at high speed, jovially encouraging me to step up the pace. Five minutes later I saw him chilling at the Dreiländereck near Basel, the border between Germany, Swiss and France.

I joined him in his break. He was recording several personal voice mails for friends & family in Moscow. In his fast-spoken Russian I could recognize some city names. In between we talked with my broken German - English was not an option. I learned that he had started his trip in the Netherlands and was heading to Rome. He did an astonishing 150km per day. When he pulled a Red Bull out of his bag to share it with me, I got a feel for how he managed to do that.
